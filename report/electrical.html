1. Overview of the Electrical System

The electrical subsystem delivers and regulates power throughout Jellybot, ensuring stable and efficient energy distribution to all components. It integrates the battery system, voltage regulators, and power management circuits to support long-duration underwater operation. Safety features such as overcurrent protection and waterproof connectors enhance reliability under varying load conditions.

2. Electrical System

In accordance with our design specifications(3a), the electrical subsystem was developed to operate within a 12–24 V tethered power supply and a maximum system load of approximately 30 W. The final design utilises two 12 V motors delivering an airflow rate of 0.6 L/min each, resulting in a combined maximum power consumption of 32 W. This aligns closely with the intended power budget and satisfies the required voltage and current stability validated through load testing.

3. Wiring Diagram

Figure 1 : Wiring Diagram of our setup

The electrical system consists of two air pumps, two solenoid valves, an Arduino microcontroller, and two motor drivers. The motor drivers are used to control both the pumps and solenoids, enabling precise timing and coordination of airflow. The solenoid valves regulate the direction of air—either channeling it into the silicone tentacles for inflation or releasing it into the atmosphere for deflation. This setup allows the Arduino to manage the inflation-deflation cycle automatically, ensuring consistent pressure control and repeatable actuation behavior during testing.

4. Tests & Results

To ensure compliance with our design specifications for pneumatic actuation, we are currently conducting tentacle pressure and bending tests. These tests evaluate the system’s ability to generate sufficient internal pressure and produce the required bending deformation in the silicone tentacles. Specifically, we are verifying that the tentacles achieve the target pressure range of 120–170 kPa design specifications(2a) without rupture and demonstrate stable pressure cycling behaviour.
Additionally, we are assessing the pulse frequency performance, ensuring that the actuation follows the required 0.16–0.25 Hz range (4–6 s period) as specified in design specifications(2b), where silicone tentacles are expected to reach maximum deformation within 2–3 seconds and achieve maximum pressure at approximately 2 seconds.
The testing scope includes examining the effect of using one versus two pumps on inflation performance, characterising the relationship between internal air pressure and bending angle, and verifying pressure buildup, equilibrium behaviour, and actuation speed. These evaluations collectively confirm whether the pneumatic subsystem meets the functional and safety requirements defined in our design specifications and also to confirm that the system can achieve controlled, repeatable bending under pneumatic pressure which is a key requirement for soft robotic actuation.

4.1 Pressure Testing for Optimal Actuation (1 Pump vs 2 Pump)

A series of controlled tests were carried out to identify the optimal internal pressure required for the pneumatic actuators to achieve maximum expansion and responsive bending. Various configurations were evaluated by adjusting the supply voltage and altering the number of active DC air pumps and solenoid valves, as shown in table 2. Internal pressure was recorded using a digital barometric sensor integrated into the actuator chamber. The pneumatic pressure was generated using two motor-driven air pumps, with an Arduino microcontroller managing the timing and control sequence. Two solenoid valves were implemented, one to route airflow into the tentacle and another to vent air to the atmosphere, enabling precise inflation and deflation during testing.

Conclusion:
The blue line represents max pressure recorded at 1173hPa using 2 pumps at around 2 seconds. The red line represents max pressure recorded at 917hPa using 1 pump at around 1 seconds. Within 1 seconds, the pressure inside all tentacles has reached its peak, as shown in figure 2.
These results highlight that pressure determines the final stiffness of the pneumatic tentacles, while airflow rate governs the speed of actuation. Increasing the number of pumps does not linearly double the output pressure, as pneumatic pressure is not strictly additive. Instead, adding pumps primarily increases the airflow rate, leading to faster inflation and improved responsiveness, rather than a proportional increase in maximum pressure.

Shortcomings:
The testing process revealed several limitations. The pressure measurements exhibited an immediate jump, making it difficult to capture gradual rises in the inflation profile. Additionally, inconsistencies in the silicone mixture caused uneven mechanical properties across samples, resulting in excessive inflation and localized ballooning, which increased the risk of rupture.

Future improvements:
To address these limitations, future work will involve separating the pump outputs into independent channels for each tentacle, rather than combining them into a shared line, allowing for more consistent and controlled pressure delivery. Further improvements include experimenting with alternative silicone formulations, as the current prototypes use pure Ecoflex 00-30, which may not provide the optimal balance between elasticity and structural stability.

4.3 Bending Angle of Actuators (1 Pump vs 2 Pump)

A series of controlled experiments were conducted to evaluate the bending angle of the silicone tentacles and to investigate how the number of active pumps influences both the bending angle and actuation dynamics, in accordance with the pulse frequency requirements (design specifications 2b) where maximum formation is expected within 2–3 seconds and peak pressure occurs at 2 seconds.
As shown in Figure 3, the tentacle at 0 degree bend is displayed on the right, while the tentacle at maximum bend is shown on the left.

Conclusion:
As shown in figure 5 and 6 we can see that with 1 pump, the tentacles achieved a maximum bend angle of 41 Degree in 1 second & with 2 pump, the tentacles achieved a maximum bend angle of 57 Degree in 1.2 seconds. Interestingly, the bending angle peaks at around the same time the internal pressure reaches its maximum.

Shortcomings:
The material compliance of the silicone limits further bending beyond a certain pressure, with thicker silicone resulting in smaller bend angles due to higher stiffness. Inconsistencies in the silicone mixture can also lead to excessive inflation, causing localized ballooning and increasing the risk of rupture. Additionally, the displacement of the Jellybot varies with bend angle, highlighting the need for precise measurement and control of actuation to ensure predictable motion.

Future improvements:
Future work should focus on standardizing the silicone mixture to reduce variability in mechanical properties and minimize the risk of ballooning. Testing different silicone thicknesses or alternative formulations may help achieve larger bend angles while maintaining structural integrity. Furthermore, systematic measurement of Jellybot displacement at each bend angle should be conducted to better understand the relationship between actuation pressure and resulting motion, enabling improved control and performance.

4.4 Comparison of pressure vs bend angle

Next, we analyse the relationship between internal pneumatic pressure and the resulting bend angle of the silicone tentacles. By plotting pressure versus bend angle, we aim to characterise how variations in actuation pressure translate into mechanical deformation. This analysis provides insight into the responsiveness of the tentacles, the influence of pump configurations on bending performance, and the limits imposed by the material properties of the silicone. The results will help evaluate whether the tentacle actuation meets the design specifications for both bending range and controllability.

Figure 7 : Non linear comparison of pressure vs bend angle

Conclusion:
When plotting pressure vs bend angle, we observed a non linear relationship between the pressure within the tentacles vs the degree of the bent of the tentacles. The tentacle resists bending at low pressures, it then bends rapidly after a certain threshold and finally it saturates at a maximum angle. The nonlinear response is influenced by the material properties, including the thickness and elasticity of the silicone, which affect the threshold pressure and the rate of bending. Thicker or stiffer silicone results in smaller maximum bend angles and requires higher pressure to reach the rapid bending region. Additionally, variations in the silicone mixture and fabrication inconsistencies can lead to differences in bending behavior between tentacles. Understanding this relationship is critical for predicting actuation performance, designing control strategies, and ensuring that the tentacles operate safely within their material limits while meeting the design specifications.

4.5 Key Takeaways

Lessons Learnt:

  • Pressure governs stiffness, but flow rate governs actuation speed. Increasing number of pumps used does not directly double the pressure as pneumatic pressure is not additive. It only increase the air flow rate which meant faster inflation speed.
  • The material compliance of the silicone also limites further bending beyond a certain pressure, with thicker silicone materials results in smaller bend angles due to higher stiffness.

Shortcomings:

  • Measurements show immediate pressure jump, hard to capture gradual rises in the pressure recording
  • Inconsistency in the mixture of the silicone materials will cause tendency for excessive inflation which in turn caused silicone balloning, increasing the risk of rupturing.

Future improvements:

  • Separate pump outputs to independent tentacle channels instead of combining them.
  • Test with different silicone material mixture, currently we are using Ecoflex 00-30 purely.
  • Test the displacement of the Jellybot for each bend angle, and record the distance it moves with regards to the bend angle.

5. Future Improvements

The development process began with selecting the appropriate microcontroller and identifying the required sensors. Following this, the initial circuit schematic was designed and the wiring layout was planned. The system diagram was then finalized alongside the selection of a suitable power source. Early circuit block testing was conducted and any necessary electronic components were ordered. Key components were first assembled on a breadboard, with perfboards prepared for more permanent mounting. The prototype circuit was soldered together, including the attachment of drivers and sensors, and subsequently wired to the microcontroller while early waterproofing measures were applied. Sensors and drivers were integrated and functionally tested before refining the circuit layout and tuning power distribution. All wiring was then verified, and a dry-run prototype test was performed. Waterproofing improvements and power flow adjustments were made prior to a full system test, which included telemetry collection and logging. Finally, all integration learnings and circuit diagrams were compiled into the project report.
