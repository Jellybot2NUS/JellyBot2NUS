Overview

The electrical subsystem of the Jellybot is a multi-tier power and communication architecture designed to bridge high-current actuation with precision digital control. At its core, the system regulates power for a dual-Raspberry Pi configuration, coordinating the Master–Slave communication required for remote underwater surveillance.

To meet the specific demands of coral reef monitoring, the subsystem prioritises actuation stability over raw thrust. It integrates a BlueOS Flight Navigator with a 2-pump and 2-solenoid valve array, allowing for a dampened response characterised by a time constant τ. This configuration ensures that the electrical load remains optimised at 76.8 W, extending mission endurance to 3.1 hours.

Tests & Results

To determine the optimal configuration for the coral detection mission, two key tests were conducted: a pump configuration test evaluating actuation performance at the target 71° bend angle, and an energy consumption test quantifying mission endurance across all configurations.

4.1 Pump Configuration — 2 vs 4 Pumps

To demonstrate that a 2-pump configuration can match the steady-state performance of a 4-pump system, both setups were evaluated at maximum actuation. A maximum bend angle of ~71° was confirmed using HX710 pressure sensing at 206 kPa — sufficient to overcome hydrostatic pressure beyond 5 m depth.

The bend angle tracking software was used to monitor deformation underwater in real time. Both configurations were driven until reaching steady-state at 71°, but with differing transient behaviour.

Both systems achieved steady-state at 71°. The 4-pump system reached it in 0.7 s (τ ≈ 0.45 s), while the 2-pump system reached it in 1.4 s (τ ≈ 1.1 s, measured at 63.2% of steady-state = 45.1°). The slower 2-pump response introduces a natural dampening effect that proves advantageous for depth stability.

Key Finding

Both configurations reach the same steady-state bend angle (71°). The difference lies in the transient response: 4-pump reaches steady-state in 0.7 s vs 1.4 s for 2-pump. The 2-pump system's higher time constant τ provides a mechanical low-pass filter, suppressing abrupt buoyancy shifts that degrade imaging quality.

Shortcomings

The slower 2-pump actuation reduces peak propulsion speed. Silicone material compliance limits further bending beyond the current maximum pressure, and inconsistencies in silicone mixture can affect repeatability.

Future Improvements

Implement a hybrid control logic: operate on 2 pumps for precision hover/detection mode and reserve 4-pump capacity for high-thrust/extraction mode. Standardise the silicone mixture and test alternative formulations for improved compliance.

Table 5 — Depth Deviation Comparison During Active Depth Control

The 2-pump system's slower time constant (τ) effectively acts as a "dampener," allowing finer granular control over buoyancy. This reduction in vertical jitter is essential for maintaining the constant focal length required for automated coral detection algorithms. A higher τ describes a more sluggish but smoother system — in the coral reef context, this is a feature, not a limitation.

Time Constant Interpretation

A lower τ (4-pump, τ ≈ 0.45 s) indicates a fast system reaching target displacement rapidly — ideal for propulsion but causing aggressive, jerky movements that make depth control difficult. A higher τ (2-pump, τ ≈ 1.1 s) produces a smoother buoyancy transition, filtering out high-frequency jitter and enabling tighter depth precision.

4.4 Conclusion

Comparative Summary

The 4-pump system offered a superior time constant (τ = 0.45 s) and peak mean velocity of 10 cm/s, but induced high-magnitude depth jitter of ±8 cm. The 2-pump System 3 architecture achieved a stable mean velocity of 3.05 cm/s and reduced depth oscillations to ±2 cm — a 4× improvement in depth precision.

The adoption of the 2-pump configuration (System 3) is justified by three key engineering factors:

Mission-Specific Stability

The higher time constant of the 2-pump setup provides a mechanical "low-pass filter" effect, dampening abrupt buoyancy shifts. This stability is critical for capturing high-resolution, blur-free imagery of coral reefs.

Logistical & Thermal Efficiency

Reducing the pump count significantly improved the volumetric efficiency within the waterproof Pelican housing. This allowed for better airflow and heat dissipation for the dual-Raspberry Pi setup, preventing thermal throttling during extended missions.

Extended Operational Endurance

System 3 optimises power draw to 76.8 W, extending mission runtime to 3.1 hours — a 15% improvement in total survey area per deployment compared to the 2.7-hour limit of the 4-pump system.

While System 2 offers maximum theoretical runtime (3.2 hr), System 3 was adopted as the final architecture. 06

Future Work

Future iterations will implement a hybrid control logic. By utilising the space-saving and thermal benefits discovered in this phase, the system can be engineered to operate on 2 pumps for precision "Hover/Detection Mode" while reserving 4-pump capacity for a "High-Thrust/Extraction Mode."

This dual-state capability, backed by the mathematical models derived in this report, will ensure the Jellybot remains a versatile tool for long-term underwater environmental monitoring. Integrating feedback sensors directly into the tentacles would further provide real-time pressure data, enabling dynamic monitoring and closed-loop pressure control to prevent over-inflation.

Planned Improvements
  • Hybrid 2/4-pump mode switching for hover vs. extraction missions
  • Closed-loop pressure feedback via in-tentacle sensors
  • Standardised silicone formulations beyond pure Ecoflex 00-30
  • Systematic displacement measurement per bend angle for improved control mapping
