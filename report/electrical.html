Overview of the Electrical System

The electrical subsystem provides and regulates power throughout Jellybot, supporting reliable operation of its actuation and control components. It coordinates the operation of pumps, valves, and control electronics to manage airflow and ensure consistent actuation behaviour.

Designed to maintain stable voltage and current under varying loads, the subsystem incorporates essential safety and protection features to ensure durability and dependable performance during testing and operation.

02

Electrical System

In accordance with our design specifications (3a), the electrical subsystem was developed to operate within a 12–24 V tethered power supply and a maximum system load of approximately 30 W. The final design utilises two 12 V motors delivering an airflow rate of 0.6 L/min each, resulting in a combined maximum power consumption of 32 W.

03

Wiring Diagram

The electrical system consists of two air pumps, two solenoid valves, an Arduino microcontroller, and two motor drivers. The motor drivers control both the pumps and solenoids, enabling precise timing and coordination of airflow. The solenoid valves regulate the direction of air — either channeling it into the silicone tentacles for inflation or releasing it to atmosphere for deflation.

This setup allows the Arduino to manage the inflation-deflation cycle automatically, ensuring consistent pressure control and repeatable actuation behaviour during testing.

04

Tests & Results

To ensure compliance with our design specifications for pneumatic actuation, we are currently conducting tentacle pressure and bending tests. These tests evaluate the system's ability to generate sufficient internal pressure and produce the required bending deformation in the silicone tentacles. Specifically, we are verifying that the tentacles achieve the target pressure range of 120–170 kPa design specifications (2a) without rupture and demonstrate stable pressure cycling behaviour.

Additionally, we are assessing the pulse frequency performance, ensuring actuation follows the required 0.16–0.25 Hz range (4–6 s period) as specified in design specifications (2b), where silicone tentacles are expected to reach maximum deformation within 2–3 seconds and peak pressure at approximately 2 seconds.

The testing scope includes examining the effect of using one versus two pumps on inflation performance, characterising the relationship between internal air pressure and bending angle, and verifying pressure buildup, equilibrium behaviour, and actuation speed.

4.1 Pressure Testing for Optimal Actuation

A series of controlled tests were carried out to identify the optimal internal pressure required for the pneumatic actuators to achieve maximum expansion and responsive bending. Various configurations were evaluated by adjusting the supply voltage and altering the number of active DC air pumps and solenoid valves. Internal pressure was recorded using a digital barometric sensor integrated into the actuator chamber.

Key Finding

Comparing to the target 120–170 kPa pressure range, our tests achieved only 117.3 kPa. Increasing the number of pumps does not linearly double output pressure — it primarily increases airflow rate, leading to faster inflation rather than proportionally higher maximum pressure.

Shortcomings

Pressure measurements exhibited an immediate jump, making it difficult to capture gradual rises in the inflation profile. Inconsistencies in the silicone mixture caused uneven mechanical properties, resulting in excessive inflation and localised ballooning, increasing rupture risk.

Future Improvements

Separate pump outputs into independent channels per tentacle rather than a shared line. Experiment with alternative silicone formulations beyond pure Ecoflex 00-30 to better balance elasticity and structural stability.

4.3 Bending Angle of Actuators

A series of controlled experiments were conducted to evaluate the bending angle of the silicone tentacles and investigate how the number of active pumps influences both bending angle and actuation dynamics, in accordance with pulse frequency requirements (design specifications 2b) where maximum deformation is expected within 2–3 seconds and peak pressure at approximately 2 seconds.

Key Finding

With 1 pump the tentacles achieved a maximum bend angle of 41° in 1 second; with 2 pumps, 57° in 1.2 seconds. The bending angle peaks at around the same time internal pressure reaches its maximum. Comparing to the target 4–6 s period per cycle, our tests achieved only a 1–2 s period.

Shortcomings

Material compliance of the silicone limits further bending beyond a certain pressure. Thicker silicone produces smaller bend angles due to higher stiffness. Inconsistencies in silicone mixture can lead to localised ballooning and rupture risk.

Future Improvements

Standardise the silicone mixture to reduce variability in mechanical properties. Test different silicone thicknesses and alternative formulations for larger bend angles with structural integrity. Systematically measure Jellybot displacement at each bend angle for improved control.

4.4 Comparison of Pressure vs Bend Angle

We analyse the relationship between internal pneumatic pressure and the resulting bend angle of the silicone tentacles. By plotting pressure versus bend angle, we characterise how variations in actuation pressure translate into mechanical deformation — providing insight into tentacle responsiveness, pump configuration influence, and material property limits.

Figure 7 — Non-linear Comparison of Pressure vs Bend Angle

Conclusion

A non-linear relationship was observed between internal pressure and bending angle. The tentacle resists bending at low pressures, then bends rapidly after a certain threshold, finally saturating at a maximum angle. The nonlinear behaviour means simple open-loop pressure commands are unreliable; control gains must adapt or incorporate feedback mechanisms to maintain accuracy. Understanding this relationship is essential for predicting actuation performance and developing effective control strategies.

4.5 Key Takeaways

Lessons Learnt

Pressure governs stiffness, but flow rate governs actuation speed. Increasing the number of pumps does not directly double the pressure — pneumatic pressure is not additive; it only increases airflow rate, meaning faster inflation but not proportionally higher maximum pressure. The material compliance of the silicone also limits further bending beyond a certain pressure, with thicker silicone resulting in smaller bend angles due to higher stiffness.

Shortcomings

Measurements show an immediate pressure jump, making it hard to capture gradual rises in pressure recording. Inconsistency in the silicone mixture causes a tendency for excessive inflation, resulting in ballooning and increasing the risk of rupturing.

Future Improvements

Separate pump outputs to independent tentacle channels instead of combining them. Test with different silicone material mixtures, as current prototypes use pure Ecoflex 00-30. Test the displacement of Jellybot for each bend angle and record the distance it moves with respect to bending angle.

05

Future Improvements

To enhance the performance of the pneumatic system, future iterations could incorporate higher air-flow pumps to achieve faster and more consistent tentacle actuation. Coupling this with an improved control algorithm would allow finer timing adjustments, ensuring smoother and more precise movements.

Additionally, integrating feedback sensors directly into the tentacles would provide real-time pressure data, enabling the system to dynamically monitor and adjust internal pressure. This closed-loop approach would improve reliability, prevent over-inflation, and allow the tentacles to achieve the desired bending more accurately.
