3. Buoyancy Tests

Neutral buoyancy is achieved when the upward force known as Buoyant Force = downward force also known as Weight as shown below.

Objective :

To verify that the Jellybot housing (i) maintains an upright (vertical) attitude in water and (ii) achieves neutral or slightly negative buoyancy so it remains submerged at a controlled depth rather than floating at the surface. This verification is a pre-requisite before open water trials to avoid uncontrolled floating and to confirm a stable trim suitable for imaging and sensing.

3.1 Procedure

The following steps outline the procedure used to assess the buoyancy and stability of the housing:

  1. Remove tentacles and flaps.
  2. Wrap housing with two plastic layers and remove as much trapped air as possible.
  3. Place the chosen ballast at the base of the housing to lower its centre of gravity (CG).
  4. Lower the housing into the water tank, release, and allow it to settle.
  5. Record observations such as:
    • Attitude (Uprightness)
    • Submergence distance from surface of water to top end-cap
    • Any other behavior
  6. Repeat experiment as needed.

3.2 Acceptance Criteria

Buoyancy: Neutral / Slightly Negative — The housing remains submerged without rising to the surface.

Stability: The housing settles upright and stays that way; no tendency to roll.

3.3 Results and Observations

Observations were made for each ballast configuration to evaluate buoyancy behavior and orientation stability.

  • No Added Mass (Fig. 3): Floated horizontally at the water surface; positively buoyant and not upright.
  • Added Mass = 1.7 kg (Fig. 4): Housing settled upright and submerged distance = 17–32 cm; neutrally buoyant with attitude stable.
  • Added Mass = 2 kg (Fig. 5): Housing settled upright and submerged distance = 29–44 cm with slight sinking tendency; neutrally buoyant with attitude stable.

4. Additional Calculations and Interpretations

Buoyant Force:

Fb = ρ × g × Vdisp,housing+dome

ρ = 1 g/cm³ (density of fresh water)

Vdisp,housing+dome 
= π(Ro²)(Htotal) + 2π(Rcap,out² - Ro²)(Hcap) + 68.66
= π(5.7²)(Htotal) + 2π(6.15² - 5.7²)(5.3) + 68.66
= [102.07Htotal + 177.577 + 68.66] cm³
= [102.07(15) + 177.577 + 68.66] cm³
= 1777.287 cm³

Therefore, Fb = 1(g)(1777.287) = g1777.287 N

To achieve neutral buoyancy, the buoyant force must equal the weight: Fb = W = mg.

Interpretation:

  • If Fb > W → Positive buoyancy → Floats to surface.
  • If Fb < W → Negative buoyancy → Sinks to bottom.

4.1 Mass of Housing + Dome

Mhousing+dome 
= [Vpvc × Density] + Mdome
= [13.823Htotal + 158.51](1.38) + 15
= [19.076Htotal + 218.75 + 15] g
= [19.076(15) + 218.75 + 15] g
= 519.89 g

Therefore, W = mg = g519.89 N
(Fb > W → Positively buoyant → Floats)

4.2 Additional Mass for Neutral Buoyancy

Mass needed for neutral buoyancy:
= g1777.287 - g519.89
= 1257.397 g ≈ 1.26 kg

Since W > Fb (2219.89 > 1777.287), 
prototype should sink — however it hovered between 17–32 cm depth,

Fb' = ρ × g × (Vdisp,housing+dome + Vair_in_plastic)

g(1777.287 + Vair_in_plastic) = g2219.89
Vair_in_plastic = 2219.89 - 1777.287 = 442.603 cm³

4.4 Added Mass = 2 kg

Total Mass = [519.89 + 2000] g = 2519.89 g
W = g2519.89 N

Since W > Fb (2519.89 > 1777.287),
prototype should sink, yet hovered between 29–44 cm depth,
again maintaining neutral buoyancy due to displaced air volume.

Fb' = ρ × g × (1777.287 + Vair_in_plastic)
Fb' = W (neutral buoyancy)

g(1777.287 + Vair_in_plastic) = g2519.89
Vair_in_plastic = 2519.89 - 1777.287 = 742.603 cm³

These results confirm that as additional mass increases, the amount of trapped air displacing water also increases, leading to neutral buoyancy achieved at deeper depths due to larger submerged air volumes.

5. Propulsion Test (Static)

5.1 Objective

The objective of this test was to determine the optimal actuation duration for the pneumatic system controlling the tentacles and central flap of the jellyfish prototype. This duration should achieve the most effective expansion and contraction cycle — maximizing stroke volume and responsiveness — without causing excessive delay or material creep. The findings from this static (air-based) test would be used as the baseline setting for subsequent dynamic (in-water) propulsion experiments.

5.2 Test Approach

The static propulsion test aimed to evaluate how the actuator’s tentacles and flap behave under different inflation times. Since pneumatic actuators follow a time-dependent pressure-filling curve, the inflation period affects both the amount of expansion (stroke size) and the time required for deflation (reset speed). Three actuation durations were tested: 4 seconds, 5 seconds, and 6 seconds of valve-open time. Each trial followed the same procedure: the tentacles and flap were allowed to fully inflate and then deflate naturally once the valve closed. The entire sequence was recorded on video.

5.3 Test Setup

The prototype was placed in a controlled environment with air supplied through the same pneumatic system used for water tests. The valve timing was controlled manually at 4 s, 5 s, and 6 s intervals for three separate iterations. Videos were captured for each test.

5.4 Results and Observations

4-Second Actuation:
The tentacles began expanding but did not reach their full curvature or maximum surface displacement before the air supply stopped. Inflation appeared incomplete, and the flap movement was visibly smaller compared to the other two trials. Deflation occurred quickly, but the reduced expansion meant the overall stroke per cycle was small.

5-Second Actuation:
The tentacles reached near-maximum expansion around the 5-second mark, displaying strong bending and full flap engagement. The inflation curve approached a plateau, indicating that the actuator had reached almost full volume without excessive dwell. Deflation was smooth and relatively quick, returning close to the original shape with minimal lag. Visually, this cycle appeared the most balanced and repeatable among the three.

6-Second Actuation:
The actuator reached full expansion slightly earlier (around 5 seconds) and remained at the plateau during the final second of inflation. The extended dwell caused minimal extra displacement but introduced a slower deflation response. The tentacles held their inflated shape longer before returning, suggesting increased internal pressure retention or minor viscoelastic creep of the silicone material.

5.5 Interpretations

At 4 seconds, inflation was incomplete, resulting in smaller stroke volume and weaker thrust potential.

At 6 seconds, the system operated in the region of diminishing returns — extra inflation time produced minimal additional movement but increased material stress and delayed reset.

The 5-second actuation achieved approximately 95% of the maximum displacement while maintaining fast deflation and consistent cycle timing. This balance provides the highest effective stroke per unit time, making it the most efficient duration for propulsion.

In essence, 5 seconds of actuation filled the tentacles and flap to near their maximum useful expansion without overinflating or wasting pneumatic energy. It also preserved structural responsiveness for repeated cycles, which is crucial for continuous swimming motions in dynamic water tests. Therefore, the 5 s setting was selected as the optimal actuation period for subsequent underwater propulsion experiments.
