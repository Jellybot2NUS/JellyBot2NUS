Conclusion

After comparing the four actuator options, the pneumatic system stood out as the most balanced and practical choice, with the highest overall score of 4.10. HASEL actuators can closely mimic the movement of real jellyfish, and Shape-Memory Alloys (SMA) are simple to build, but both have clear downsides—HASEL and Dielectric Elastomers (DE) need very high voltages to run, and SMA wastes energy through heating and wears out quickly. Pneumatic actuators, by contrast, operate safely at low voltage, use harmless air or fluid, and are much cheaper to produce. Although building and sealing the tubing system takes some effort, the process is well understood and manageable with standard lab tools. In short, pneumatic silicone actuation offers the best mix of realistic motion, safety, affordability, and environmental compatibility, making it the most suitable option for powering our jellyfish robot underwater.

3. Calculations Data

Calculations Made to Achieve Neutral Buoyancy

To achieve neutral buoyancy, the weight of the displaced water must equal the weight of the prototype. Under freshwater conditions (ρ = 1 g/cm³), this simplifies to: 

      Volume of Displaced Water = Mass of Prototype

Prototype 1 CAD Design

We calculate the volume of displaced water by summing the volumes of all external components: PVC housing, dome, flap, and tentacles.

PVC Housing

The PVC housing includes the pipe and two end caps. The pipe height (Htotal) is unknown. 

      PVC Pipe:
        Density = 1.38 g/cm³
        Outer Radius, Rₒ = 5.7 cm
        Inner Radius, Rᵢ = 5.3 cm
      
      End Caps (x2):
        Density = 1.38 g/cm³
        Outer Radius, Rcap,out = 6.15 cm
        Inner Radius, Rcap,in = 5.75 cm
        Height, Hcap = 5.3 cm
      
      Unknown:
        Htotal = (2 × Hcap) + Hexposed,pipe

Acrylic Dome

The acrylic dome is hemispherical. The volume of a hemisphere is given by: 

      Vdome = (2/3)πr³
      r = 3.2 cm
      Vdome ≈ 68.66 cm³
      Mdome = 15 g

Silicone Flap and Tentacles

The flap and eight tentacles are made from Ecoflex 00-30 and Dragonskin 00-20 silicone. 

      Flap:
        Density = 1.07 g/cm³
        Rout = 15.2 cm
        Rin = 5.7 cm
        Thickness = 0.3 cm
        Volume = π(Rout² - Rin²)(Thickness)
                = 187.09 cm³
        Mass = 1.07 × 187.09 = 200.19 g
      
      Tentacles (×8):
        Length = 9.5 cm, Width = 2 cm, Height = 2 cm, Thickness = 0.2 cm
        Full Volume (8×) = 9.5 × 2 × 2 × 8 = 304 cm³
        Inner Air Volume = 190.46 cm³
        Silicone Volume = 304 - 190.46 = 113.54 cm³
        Mass = 113.54 × 1.07 = 121.49 g

External Displaced Volume

Since we have the dimensions of all components that contribute to the volume of water displaced, we can start calculating the volume of these components and therefore the volume of displaced water. The volume of the acrylic dome is as follows: 

      Vdome = 68.66 cm³
      Vflap = 187.09 cm³
      8 × Vtentacle = 304 cm³
      
      Total (less PVC Housing):
      Vfixed = 68.66 + 187.09 + 304 = 559.75 cm³
      
      PVC Housing:
      Vdisp,housing = π(Rₒ²)(Htotal) + 2π(Rcap,out² - Rₒ²)(Hcap)
                               = 102.07Htotal + 177.58 cm³
      
      Total Displaced Volume:
      Vdisp,total = 559.75 + 102.07Htotal + 177.58
                             = 102.07Htotal + 737.33 cm³

Total Mass Calculation

Now that we have our algebraic equation for the volume of displaced water, we now have to find the mass of all components that contributes to the total mass of the prototype. 

      Mdome = 15 g
      Mflap = 200.19 g
      8 × Mtentacle = 121.49 g
      Minternal = 605 g
      Mfixed = 941.68 g
      
      PVC Housing:
      Vpvc = π(Rₒ² - Rᵢ²)(Htotal) + 2π(Rcap,out² - Rcap,in²)(Hcap)
                      = 13.823Htotal + 158.51 cm³
      Mpvc = (13.823Htotal + 158.51) × 1.38
                      = 19.08Htotal + 218.75 g
      
      Total Mass:
      Mtotal = 941.68 + 19.08Htotal + 218.75
                        = 19.08Htotal + 1160.43 g

3.1 Theoretical Calculations

To achieve neutral buoyancy, the total mass (Mtotal) must be equal to the total volume of water displaced (Vdisp,total). 

Mtotal = Vdisp,total
19.076Htotal + 1160.43 = 102.07Htotal + 737.33

-737.33 + 1160.43 = (102.07 - 19.076)Htotal
423.1 = 82.994Htotal

Htotal = 423.1 / 82.994
Htotal ≈ 5.10 cm

However, it is not possible for the height to be 5.10 cm due to the internal components and structural compatibility. Hence, to maintain neutral buoyancy, we must increase the mass required. 

Mneeded = [82.994Htotal - 423.1] g

For our Jellybot of Htotal = 15 cm:
Mneeded = [82.994(15) - 423.1] g
Mneeded ≈ 821.81 g

4. Buoyancy Tests

Neutral buoyancy is achieved when the upward force known as Buoyant Force = downward force also known as Weight as shown below.

Objective :

To verify that the Jellybot housing (i) maintains an upright (vertical) attitude in water and (ii) achieves neutral or slightly negative buoyancy so it remains submerged at a controlled depth rather than floating at the surface. This verification is a pre-requisite before open water trials to avoid uncontrolled floating and to confirm a stable trim suitable for imaging and sensing.

Buoyant Force (Fb) = ρ x g x Volume displaced
Weight, W = mg
Therefore, Fb = W
Assuming fresh water density, ρ = 1g/cm³
Gravitational acceleration, g cancels out from both equations.
Thus, Vdisplaced = m to achieve neutral buoyanc

4.1 Determining the Additional Mass Required

 
    Buoyancy Analysis of JellyBot
    Naturally JellyBot is positively buoyant as proven from the calculations
    Theoretical Mass, m = 19.076Htotal + 1160.43g
                        = 19.076(15) + 1160.43g
                        = 1446.57g
    Theoretical Vdisp,total, Vdisplaced = 102.07Htotal + 737.33cm³
                                                             = 102.07(15) + 737.33cm³
                                                             = 2268.38cm³
    Since Fb, g2268.38N > W, g1446.57N, JellyBot is positively buoyant.
    We have also confirmed this theory as shown in Fig. 5
    Based on theoretical calculations, additional mass required for neutral buoyancy:
    [g2268.38N − g1446.57N] / g = 821.81g
    Since internal components (mass = 605g) are removed,
    total theoretical additional mass required = 821.81 + 605
                                               = 1426.81g
    However, the actual mass of JellyBot weighed in at 1603.45g as shown in Fig. 6
    In actuality, JellyBot is heavier by 1603.45 − 1446.57 = 156.88g
    Therefore, actual additional mass required = 1426.81 − 156.88
                                               = 1269.93g

4.2 Buoyancy Test

4.21 Neutral Buoyancy Test

As per the above calculations, additional mass added:
1kg sandbag + 200g iron mass (mass = 1.2kg)

Observations and Interpretations

JellyBot remained neutrally buoyant, hovering at the position where it was submerged in the water as shown below. This indicated Buoyant Force = Weight (Fb = W).

However, JellyBot did not remain completely upright and tilted slightly.

Even though the Center of Gravity (CG) was below the Center of Buoyancy (CB), they were laterally off which caused JellyBot to tilt slightly until the vertical line of CB passed through CG.

4.22 Negative Buoyancy Test

Additional mass added:
1kg sandbag + 500g iron mass (mass = 1.5kg)

Observations and Interpretations

JellyBot sank to the bottom of the tank which makes sense as this indicated Buoyant Force < Weight (Fb < W).

JellyBot also overturned as it sank.

This was most likely due to the Center of Gravity (CG) being above the Center of Buoyancy (CB), causing it to be unstable and overturn until the masses shifted below.

4.4 Shortcomings

Currently our JellyBot is not able to descend to depths on its own. It would require manual assistance to place the bot at our desired depth.

Tests were conducted in stable freshwater. Considering in reality there is seawater current, we would require JellyBot to have positive stability. This is to ensure self correcting capabilities when faced against the water current force.

4.5 Future Recommendations

To combat the depth issue, we are considering a Variable Buoyancy System (VBS). With a VBS, we would be able to reach desired depths by pumping in water (Fb < W), causing the JellyBot to be negatively buoyant and sink. When approaching the desired depth for surveillance, the pump would release the water so that the weight of the JellyBot equals the buoyant force (Fb = W), maintaining neutral buoyancy.

To ensure positive stability, we are considering adding rigid masses or steel bearings and bolting them to the base of the cylindrical hull of JellyBot. This keeps the Center of Gravity (CG) as low as possible below the Center of Buoyancy (CB).

5. Propulsion Test

5.1 Objective

To ensure our JellyBot is able to propel upwards with a velocity of 5cm/s

5.2 Test Setup

Venue: BeeX lab water tank (freshwater).
Prototype: Full assembly with internal components and additional 553.5g sandbag + ~250g steel mass.

5.3 Propulsion Test

We submerged JellyBot to a flap depth of 40cm.
Observe the time taken for JellyBot to propel upwards during 1 cycle (4s).

5.4 Observations and Interpretations

In one cycle (4s), JellyBot propelled upward from a flap depth of 40cm to a flap depth of 30cm.

Velocity = Displacement / Time
= 10cm / 4s
= 2.5cm/s

We observed air leakage on one of the tentacles. Since our configuration connects four tentacles to a single 12V pump, the leakage affected its corresponding three tentacles. Consequently, only the remaining four tentacles managed to bend properly. The four tentacles that did not bend appeared stiff.

The motor pump operates such that during the inflation phase, surrounding air is pumped into the tentacles. During the deflation phase, the pumped air is released back into the atmosphere. Because air was unable to enter the four affected tentacles, the pump instead released the natural air within them, creating a vacuum-like effect that caused stiffness.

5.5 Shortcomings

We observed that plastic pneumatic tubes do not bond well with silicone materials. Additionally, JellyBot was unable to reach its desired velocity of 5cm/s.

5.6 Future Recommendations

To improve bonding and air sealing, we plan to replace the current plastic pneumatic tubes with silicone tubes, as silicone bonds more effectively with silicone. We also plan to adopt silicone Y-connectors, as suggested by our industry expert, Dr. Wang.
