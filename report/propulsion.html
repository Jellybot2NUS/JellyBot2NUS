System Overview

During Phase l of the JellyBot project, JellyBot 1.0 demonstrated its ability to remain neutrally buoyant under water and also displayed potential in its upwards propulsion as per the critical functions, Buoyancy and Propulsion respectively. However, from these testings, we discovered certain shortcomings which we would rectify and overcome in Phase ll

Phase ll focuses on the practicality of our project which is the ability for underwater surveillance. To achieve this, 4 properties of JellyBot 2.0 must be verified:

  1. JellyBot 2.0 must descend to desired depths without manual assistance (Achieved through its Negative Buoyancy property)
  2. JellyBot 2.0 is able to achieve upward propulsion
  3. JellyBot 2.0 is able to achieve depth control for the purpose of capturing visual data
  4. JellyBot 2.0 is able to propel horizontally

As such, 4 different types of tests were conducted, to verify the above 4 properties.

02

Achieving Negative Buoyancy

Achieving Negative Buoyancy

Negative Buoyancy is achieved when the downward Weight (W) exceeds the upward Buoyant Force (Fb).

Mass Calculation

The total dry mass of JellyBot 2.0 is 3065g (3.065kg). At standard gravity, Weight is calculated as:

W = mg ≈ 30.07N

The total displaced volume of JellyBot 2.0 is approximately 3194.5cm³. This results in a natural Buoyant Force (Fb) of 31.34N. Since W < Fb, the robot is naturally positively buoyant.[cite: 1]

Refinement for Mission Success

Theoretical neutral buoyancy required 130g of additional mass. Testing determined that 400g of added mass was the optimal threshold to achieve negative buoyancy (sinking at 3.33cm/s) while retaining the ability to propel upwards.[cite: 1]

05

Propulsion Test (NUS Swimming Pool)

To ensure high-quality visual data collection, JellyBot must maintain depth within a ±5cm tolerance. Trials at 0.5m depths confirmed mission success.[cite: 1]

Control Verification

During 10-second trials, JellyBot 2.0 successfully hovered within the required range. Trial 1 fluctuated between 50cm and 55cm, remaining within design specifications.[cite: 1]

Operational Limitation: Currently, depth control is passive (manual). The depth sensor suffers from calibration issues underwater, requiring manual controller inputs from an operator receiving surface commands.[cite: 1]
