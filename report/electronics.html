1. Overview of the Electronics System

The electronics subsystem serves as the control and sensory core of Jellybot, enabling communication, data processing, and coordination across all hardware modules. It integrates the microcontroller, sensor interfaces, and signal conditioning circuits that collect and process environmental data for navigation and control. Sensor communication is managed through digital protocols such as I²C and UART, with dedicated routines for data filtering and fusion to ensure stable readings. The subsystem is designed with waterproof connectors, organized PCB layouts, and proper grounding to minimize electrical noise and ensure reliability under high-pressure and high-humidity conditions.

2. System Components & Specifications

The listed components form the core sensing and control framework of Jellybot. Each sensor contributes a unique data stream:

  • To meet design specification 3b, the Playstation 5 Controller is used as the primary user control interface.

  • To meet design specification 4a, the SEN0311 Ultrasonic Sensor is used to measure distance to obstacles through UART.

  • To meet design specification 4b, the MPU6050 IMU is used to provide orientation through accelerometer and gyroscope readings.

  • To meet design specification 4c, the HMC5883L Magnetometer is used to offer heading information.

  • To meet design specification 4d, the Raspberry Pi Camera Module V2 is used to deliver real-time visual feedback for monitoring and analysis.

  • To meet design specification 5a, the Raspberry Pi 5 is used as the primary processing unit, coordinating communication between all onboard sensors.

Together, they enable Jellybot to perceive its environment, support navigation and stabilization algorithms, and provide essential feedback for control. The total power and mass remain minimal [Table 1], maintaining system efficiency and suitability for underwater deployment.

3. Wiring Diagram

The wiring diagram illustrates the interconnection between the sensors, controller, and peripheral devices that make up the Jellybot’s electronics subsystem. An external PS5 controller functions as the primary user interface, allowing manual control and navigation input during testing. The Raspberry Pi Camera Module 2 is directly mounted on the Raspberry Pi 5 through the CSI interface, providing live visual feedback for monitoring. The SEN0311 ultrasonic sensor communicates with the Raspberry Pi via UART, enabling accurate distance measurement, while both the MPU6050 IMU and HMC5883L magnetometer share the I²C bus for synchronized orientation and heading data acquisition. This configuration ensures efficient communication, minimal wiring complexity, and reliable real-time data exchange across all connected modules.

Objective :

The objective of this preliminary test is to evaluate the camera’s ability under simulated underwater conditions with reduced visibility. It also aims to determine the optimal distance between the camera and an object to achieve the best balance between image clarity without distortion. The findings from this test will help establish appropriate camera placement for future underwater deployments and ecological monitoring.

Findings & Limitations :

The captured images were clearest and most distinguishable at distances between 0.5 m and 1.0 m , aligning with theoretical expectations for underwater imaging resolution. Beyond this range, image clarity declined noticeably due to light scattering and refraction in the water. However, as the test utilized a simplified prop and controlled tank environment, the results may not fully replicate the complexity of actual coral reef textures or the variability of real underwater conditions such as turbidity and lighting. These preliminary findings nonetheless provide a practical reference for determining the optimal camera-to-subject distance in future field tests.

Future Testing Plans :

Future tests will focus on improving environmental realism and quantitative image analysis. Printed images will be used to better replicate natural reef textures. Still images will be captured at fixed intervals to evaluate sharpness and distortion across varying distances. Additionally, MATLAB Edge-Detection techniques will be employed to analyze image quality, focus, and detection reliability under different underwater conditions. These refinements aim to enhance the accuracy of camera calibration and the relevance of test results to real-world applications.

4.2 Navigation Testing

Motivation for Magnetometer :

Using only a 6-axis IMU (accelerometer + gyroscope) does not provide a reliable long-term heading. Yaw from a gyro is obtained by integrating angular rate, so even a small bias accumulates over time and causes drift. To obtain an absolute reference for heading, a 3-axis magnetometer is added. The magnetometer measures Earth’s magnetic field, giving a global reference for yaw; combined with accelerometer-derived roll/pitch (tilt compensation), it yields a stable compass-like heading.

The accelerometer and magnetometer data are fused mathematically to derive the orientation angles. The following equations summarize the key computations used in this system:

  • Accelerometer Conversion: Raw sensor readings are scaled using the ±2g range factor.
          
      ax = AXRAW / 16384 
      ay = AYRAW / 16384 
      az = AZRAW / 16384
  • Roll and Pitch from Accelerometer: 
          
      roll = atan2(ay, az)
      pitch = atan2(-ax, √(ay² + az²))
  • Magnetometer Calibration (Hard-Iron Offset): 
          
      m'x = mx - bx
      m'y = my - by
      m'z = mz - bz
  • Tilt Compensation: The magnetic vector is rotated into the horizontal plane to remove the influence of pitch and roll.
          
      mhx = m'xcos(pitch) + m'zsin(pitch)
      mhy = m'xsin(roll)sin(pitch) + m'ycos(roll) - m'zsin(roll)cos(pitch)
  • Heading Calculation: 
          
      heading = atan2(mhy, mhx)
      Converted to degrees and normalized to 0–360°.

This approach ensures that Jellybot maintains a consistent directional reference without accumulating yaw drift. By combining accelerometer-derived roll and pitch with magnetometer readings (tilt compensation), the system produces a stable, compass-like heading suitable for underwater navigation. Although the gyroscope is read, it is not fused into the heading calculation; therefore, the heading depends entirely on the magnetometer for long-term stability.

Objective :

This test aimed to assess the accuracy and stability of JellyBot’s magnetometer-based heading estimation, ensuring the system delivers reliable orientation data needed for directional control in underwater environments.

Test Set-up :

The test was conducted on a non-metallic table, with the JellyBot’s magnetometer module connected to the Raspberry Pi and placed flat beside a reference compass application on a smartphone. Nearby metal objects, magnets, and powered electronic devices were removed as far as practical to reduce magnetic interference.

Findings & Limitations :

The magnetometer produced heading readings that were generally consistent with the reference compass, demonstrating that the tilt-compensated magnetic heading algorithm functioned correctly. This confirmed the system’s ability to provide a basic compass-like orientation estimate. However, because the current implementation relies solely on magnetometer data (with roll and pitch derived from the accelerometer) and does not incorporate gyroscope fusion, the heading remained sensitive to magnetic disturbances in the environment. Residual interference from nearby metal objects and electronic devices occasionally caused fluctuations or small shifts in the reported heading. Unlike a fused gyro–magnetometer solution, which would stabilise short-term noise using gyro rate information, the magnetometer-only approach is more vulnerable to inconsistency and transient drift.

Future Testing Plans :

In the next phase of development, the heading system will be enhanced by incorporating a complementary filter to fuse gyroscope and magnetometer data. While the current implementation relies solely on magnetometer readings (with accelerometer-derived tilt compensation), adding gyro fusion will significantly improve short-term stability by smoothing rapid changes in orientation and reducing susceptibility to magnetic disturbances. The magnetometer will continue to provide the long-term absolute reference for yaw, while the gyroscope will supply fast, low-noise angular-rate information. By blending these signals, the complementary filter will correct gyro drift over time and produce a more stable, responsive, and reliable heading estimate—especially critical for underwater navigation where magnetic interference and motion dynamics are more challenging.

      yaw_pred   = yaw_fused_prev + gyro_z * dt
      yaw_fused  = α * yaw_pred + (1 − α) * heading_mag
      yaw_fused  = wrap360(yaw_fused)    # normalize to 0–360°

      where:
      gyro_z       = yaw rate from gyroscope (deg/s)
      dt           = time step between samples (s)

    • 5. User Interface (via Controller)

    The controller-based interface was designed to reflect the intended user journey of future JellyBot operators, offering a preliminary simulation of how the robot will be deployed, checked, and controlled during real underwater missions. Each screen—beginning with the Welcome Menu, followed by the User Guide and the staged Pre-Dive Checks—mirrors the logical workflow a user would follow: verify system readiness, confirm sensor functionality, understand operating guidelines, and only then proceed to exploration. This structure provides an early insight into the operational experience, ensuring that key interactions such as safety checks, navigation input, and live feedback handling are intuitive and aligned with real-world usage. Although the current interface is a prototype, it establishes the foundational user experience that later hardware-integrated versions will expand upon.

    Welcome Menu

    User Guide

    The Welcome Menu (top image) is the first screen the user encounters when powering on the JellyBot system. It provides an immediate overview of the robot’s status, highlighting sensor readiness and offering quick access to key actions such as the Pre-Dive Check and startup procedures. It is highlighted in the image that the programme has yet to run a pre-dive analysis. This is done intentionally. For the actual user experience, the programme would automatically run a pre-dive analysis without needing the user to perform it manually. From here, users can navigate to the User Guide (bottom image), a dedicated sub-menu designed to provide clear operational instructions. This guide outlines essential safety steps, sensor verification procedures, and basic navigation controls, ensuring that users understand how to operate the JellyBot safely and effectively before deployment.

    Ready to deploy

    The Ready to Deploy screen is the actual interface the user will see once all sensors have passed the Pre-Dive Check. This menu confirms that the camera, IMU, and ultrasonic sensor are fully operational, indicating that JellyBot is prepared for deployment. By presenting a clear readiness status, the interface ensures the user can confidently proceed with exploration knowing that all essential systems are functioning properly.

    Exploration Menu

    The Exploration Menu represents the interface that users will eventually rely on during real underwater navigation. Although not fully implemented in this prototype, it outlines the key telemetry that will be available—such as depth, heading, and IMU status—and serves as a placeholder for the live camera HUD that will be integrated in future iterations. This screen provides an early visualization of the intended user experience once JellyBot is capable of full manual exploration and real-time monitoring.

    Controller Not Connected Pop Up Menu

    Sensor not connected

    These two interfaces serve as error pop-up menus that alert the user when critical system components are not connected. The first notifies the user that the controller is missing, prompting them to plug in or pair the device before proceeding. The second highlights when one or more sensors—such as the IMU—are not detected, preventing the system from entering exploration mode. These safeguards ensure that JellyBot cannot be deployed without essential hardware operational.

    6. Summary

    Most of the sensors and interfaces demonstrated in this prototype are preliminary implementations, intended to validate core concepts such as data acquisition, basic navigation feedback, and user interaction. While they successfully provide an early functional foundation, future iterations will refine these systems with more robust hardware, improved sensor fusion, and higher-fidelity underwater performance. This lays the groundwork for a more capable and reliable JellyBot in subsequent development stages.
